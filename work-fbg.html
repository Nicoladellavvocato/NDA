FBG Sensors for Structural Health Monitoring of CFRP

BSc thesis · Politecnico di Torino · 2026 · Experimental structural testing

FBGCFRPExperimental testingMATLABData analysis

My bachelor's thesis investigates the use of fibre Bragg grating (FBG) optical sensors for structural health monitoring (SHM) of carbon-fibre-reinforced polymer (CFRP) — measuring strain inside a composite under load and looking for the signatures that mark the onset of damage.

01 Why SHM, and why FBG

Composite structures are central to modern aerospace because of their strength-to-weight ratio, but their damage modes — matrix cracking, delamination, fibre breakage — can develop internally and are not always visible from the surface. Structural health monitoring aims to instrument a structure so its condition can be assessed continuously, rather than only at scheduled inspections.

Fibre Bragg gratings are well suited to this role. A grating inscribed in an optical fibre reflects a narrow wavelength band centred on a Bragg wavelength; when the fibre is strained or its temperature changes, that reflected wavelength shifts. Reading the shift turns the fibre into a precise strain (and temperature) sensor.

  • Lightweight & small — minimal mass and intrusion, important for composite parts.
  • Immune to EMI — optical, so unaffected by electromagnetic interference.
  • Multiplexable — many gratings along a single fibre, each at its own wavelength, giving distributed sensing.
  • Embeddable — fibres can be bonded to the surface or integrated between plies of the laminate.

02 Specimen & instrumentation

The work centres on a CFRP specimen instrumented with FBG sensors, either surface-bonded or embedded so the grating experiences the strain field of the laminate. An optical interrogator illuminates the fibre and measures the reflected Bragg wavelength of each grating over time, providing the raw signal from which strain is derived.

Sensing principle

A shift in the reflected Bragg wavelength maps to mechanical strain through the grating's gauge factor. Because the wavelength also responds to temperature, separating the mechanical and thermal contributions is part of interpreting the signal correctly.

03 Test setup

The instrumented specimen is loaded in a test frame following a defined loading protocol, while the interrogator records the FBG response and the load/displacement of the machine are logged in parallel. Synchronising the optical data with the mechanical loading is what allows the wavelength response to be related to the structural state of the specimen at each instant.

04 Data analysis

The recorded wavelength data is processed to recover the engineering quantities of interest, with the analysis carried out in MATLAB:

  • Wavelength → strain — converting the measured Bragg-wavelength shift into strain using the grating's gauge factor.
  • Temperature compensation — accounting for the thermal component so the reported strain reflects mechanical loading.
  • Correlation with load — relating the strain history to the applied loading to characterise the specimen's response.
  • Damage signatures — looking for the changes in the strain response that accompany the onset and progression of damage in the composite.

05 Results & discussion

The experimental campaign demonstrates that the FBG sensors track the strain in the CFRP specimen throughout loading and that features in their response can be associated with the onset of damage in the material. The discussion focuses on how reliably the optical measurements reflect the mechanical state of the composite, the practical considerations of surface-bonded versus embedded sensing, and the factors that affect measurement quality.

Note

Detailed quantitative results, specimen specifications and the full experimental protocol are reported in the thesis document. The summary here is intentionally qualitative.

06 Conclusions & future work

The thesis supports FBG sensing as a viable approach for monitoring the structural health of composite components, combining the low mass and electromagnetic immunity of optical fibres with the ability to detect damage that conventional inspection may miss. Natural extensions include richer sensor networks for spatial coverage, more advanced processing to classify damage type, and steps toward integrating such monitoring into real aerospace structures.
